## LINKEDIN DRAFT (Hook B)

Last week I walked into a customer call with a briefing I didn't write. It knew about our 4 previous meetings, flagged a tariff issue affecting their budget, and had 12 questions I wouldn't have thought to ask.

10 minutes of reading. That used to be 40 minutes of manual research.

Last post I wrote about the Admin Tax. The gap between how fast you're growing as a founder and how flat your processes stay. Meeting prep was our worst offender. Three co-founders, 8-10 meetings a week, each one starting from scratch.

Here's what changed. We built a meeting prep workflow in Claude Code that pulls from a shared context store. Every previous meeting, every email thread, every data point we've ever collected about a company feeds into the next briefing.

The briefing isn't a CRM summary. It's actual intelligence. Prior conversation history with what was said and what's still open. Pain points we've validated across multiple calls. Specific conversation hooks tied to things the person actually cares about. Objection scenarios with counter-narratives.

The part that surprised us: we started by building skills in silos. A meeting prep tool here, an outreach tool there. Each one useful on its own. But the real unlock was when we connected them through a shared context store. What we learn in a meeting prep now feeds the next outreach. What we learn in outreach feeds the next meeting prep.

The prep compounds. Meeting 5 with a company is dramatically better than meeting 1 because there are 4 layers of context feeding it.

Our team workflow changed too. Before, Anoothi, Nandita and I would sit together for 30-40 minutes researching and brainstorming before each call. Now everyone gets a briefing PDF, reads it in 10 minutes, wraps their own POV around it, and we walk in sharper than we used to with 3x the prep time.

Is it perfect? No. Last month it flagged a company as a great fit for us. On paper everything checked out. But when we dug in, they manage their compliance internally and barely have claims volume worth automating for. Not a fit. The system missed the nuance.

But that's actually the point. It handles the 80% that's repeatable so we can spend our time on the 20% that requires judgment. Like knowing when the data says "great fit" but the reality says "pass."

That's 2-3 hours a day back for a founding team of three. Not by skipping the work. By making the work compound.
