## Full LinkedIn Post (Hook B + body)

Our head of ops told me she spent Monday mornings "getting ready to do her actual job."

That sentence broke something in me.

Because I knew exactly what she meant. I'd measured the same thing on myself 14 months ago. 60% of useful work, zero percent of it my actual job. The Admin Tax. I wrote about it then. Thought we'd fixed it.

We had not fixed it. We'd just moved it around.

She was spending her first two hours updating trackers, re-reading context from last week, copying data between tools. Formatting reports nobody read past the first paragraph. It looked like work. She was genuinely busy. None of it was her job. Her job was building partnerships and managing client onboarding. She's exceptional at it. Was doing it maybe 3 hours a day.

So we measured it across all 8 of us. Four people were spending more than half their time on work that wasn't their core function. Everyone had built their own shortcuts. Their own spreadsheets. Their own Zapier flows. An Automation Archipelago, just like Post 2 predicted. Lots of little islands of efficiency, no bridges. Each person hitting their own Context Ceiling because none of their automations could share what they knew.

The fix wasn't a new hire. Wasn't a standup about "prioritization."

It was building connective tissue so context stopped getting lost between tools. Some of it was embarrassingly simple. Auto-generating status updates from actual work output instead of asking people to write them. Pulling client data into prep docs before meetings. Routing decisions to the right person with full context instead of a Slack message that says "hey, quick question."

Some of it was harder. Systems that remember what happened in a client relationship 3 months ago without anyone writing a summary. Workflows where finishing one task sets up the next. Compound Knowledge building itself as a side effect of working.

Today it's 10%. Same 8 people. Nobody new.

Quick sidebar on why this worked as well as it did: ugly industries. 3 VCs told us to pick a "cleaner" market. Construction, insurance, compliance. Messy data, regulations that change by state. Most founders say pass. Which is exactly why the knowledge compounds into a moat nobody replicates with a seed round.

60% to 10%. That's not marginal. Our 8 people went from operating like 3 to operating like 7. Fundamentally different company.

We didn't get there by being smarter about admin. We got there by making most of it unnecessary. Making knowledge compound so the reconstruction and re-learning that used to eat everyone's mornings just... doesn't happen anymore.

We build this at lumif.ai. Not because admin is evil. Because we watched our own team lose 60% of its capacity to work that had no business being done by humans.

How much of your team's time is going to work that isn't their actual job?
