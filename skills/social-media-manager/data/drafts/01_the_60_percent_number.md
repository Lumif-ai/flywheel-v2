## LINKEDIN DRAFT (Hook A)

I tracked every minute of my work for two weeks. 60% of it was useful. None of it was my actual job.

Not meetings-about-meetings useful. Actually useful.

Updating a revenue tracker so the board deck wasn't wrong. Formatting a customer onboarding doc because the template had broken fields. Chasing down a renewal date because it lived in someone's inbox and nowhere else.

Every single task mattered. Every single task got done well. And not one of them required a CPO to do it.

.

I started calling this the Admin Tax.

Not "busywork." Not "inefficiency." The Admin Tax is useful work done by the wrong person. That's what makes it invisible. It doesn't feel wasteful while you're doing it. It feels productive. You're helping. You're unblocking. You're keeping the machine running.

But you're keeping the machine running instead of building a better machine. And that trade-off compounds every single week.

.

Here's what made me actually stop and count.

I was reviewing our product roadmap with my co-founder. He asked me why a specific feature had slipped by three weeks. I had a good answer. I'd been buried.

But buried in what?

I pulled up my calendar, my Slack history, my task list. I reconstructed two full weeks hour by hour. The math was brutal.

37 hours on admin coordination. Cross-referencing data between tools. Manually updating statuses. Writing summaries of things that had already happened so other people could be looped in. Re-finding context I'd already had two weeks ago but lost because it lived in a thread I couldn't find.

23 hours on actual product work. Architecture decisions. Customer insight synthesis. Roadmap trade-offs. The stuff I was hired to do.

60/40 split. Wrong direction.

.

The worst part isn't the ratio. It's that nobody notices.

Because the admin work is real. Boards need accurate numbers. Customers need clean onboarding. Renewals need tracking. If you stop doing it, things break.

So you keep doing it. And you get good at it. And getting good at it is the trap.

I call this the 60% Trap. You spend most of your time on work that feels productive but isn't your job. You never miss a deadline on the admin stuff. You always miss it on the strategic stuff. And because the admin is visible and the strategy is abstract, everyone thinks you're doing great.

You think you're doing great. Until you look at what actually shipped.

.

I talked to a VP of Ops at a Series B company last month. She told me she spends her mornings "getting the company ready to work." Updating dashboards. Reconciling data between HubSpot and their internal tracker. Pinging people for updates so she can compile a weekly report.

She called it "opening the shop."

She's a VP. She's opening the shop every day for three hours. By the time she's done, she has maybe two hours of deep work before the afternoon fills up with calls.

She knows it's a problem. Everyone knows it's a problem. But each individual task takes 10 minutes. You can't hire someone for forty 10-minute tasks scattered across six tools. You can't automate them because each one has just enough judgment in it that a simple Zapier trigger won't cut it.

So the VP keeps opening the shop.

.

This is what the productivity conversation gets wrong.

It's not about eliminating waste. There's very little actual waste at most startups. People are working hard on things that matter.

The problem is WHO is doing WHAT.

When your $200/hour people spend 60% of their time on $30/hour tasks, you don't have a productivity problem. You have an allocation problem. But it doesn't show up in any dashboard because the work is getting done. It shows up in what DOESN'T get done. The strategy memo that never gets written. The architecture review that keeps getting pushed. The customer pattern that nobody synthesizes because everyone's too busy updating spreadsheets.

The Admin Tax is a silent drag on your best people. Not because the work is pointless. Because the people are too expensive to be doing it.

.

After I did my time audit, I tried the usual fixes.

Hired a part-time ops person. Helped, but half the tasks needed my context to do correctly, so I ended up spending 20 minutes explaining a 10-minute task.

Set up automations. Helped, but each automation was a standalone thing. Updating the CRM didn't automatically update the board deck. Logging a customer call didn't automatically surface the insight in our next planning session. I had twelve automations and still spent 40% of my time on admin.

The issue isn't that the tools are bad. It's that useful admin requires context, and context doesn't transfer between tools by itself.

That's actually what led us to build lumif.ai the way we did. Not as another automation tool but as a layer that carries context between workflows. Because the 60% problem doesn't get solved by faster task execution. It gets solved when the context from one task flows into the next without someone manually carrying it.

But that's a different post.

.

For now, try this. Track your time for one week. Not categories, actual tasks. Then mark each one: "only I could do this" or "someone else could do this if they had the context."

Count the hours in column B.

That's your Admin Tax. And I'd bet it's closer to 60% than you think.

What's your number?
