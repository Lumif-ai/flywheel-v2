## LINKEDIN DRAFT (Hook C)

The most useful thing our automation did last month was be wrong.

We've been building internal tools to reduce our Admin Tax. One of them scores potential customers for fit before we get on a call. It researches the company, maps their structure, looks at regulatory exposure, and gives us a read on whether they'd need what we're building.

Last month it flagged a company as a strong fit. The logic was solid. They manage a nationwide network of vendors. Their enterprise clients have strict insurance and compliance requirements. They operate across multiple states with different regulations. Structurally, it looked exactly like the kind of company we serve.

We got on the call. Within 15 minutes it was clear.

They'd been in business 15 years. Maybe one or two incidents total. Their compliance tracking was basically "active or not active" at a high level. Deep compliance analysis would be overkill for them. The pain our product solves just didn't exist in their world.

The system saw the structure and missed the reality. Vendor network plus enterprise clients plus regulatory complexity doesn't automatically mean compliance pain. Without actual incidents, there's no urgency. Without urgency, there's no budget.

Here's why I think this is actually the point.

Before we built these tools, we would have spent 30-40 minutes prepping for that call manually and reached the same conclusion 15 minutes into the conversation. The system didn't save us from the bad fit. It got us to the bad fit faster.

But more importantly, that learning fed back into the system. We updated our fit criteria. "Structural similarity" alone isn't enough. Now the system looks for evidence of actual pain, not just the conditions that usually produce it.

Next time a company with similar structure shows up, the system won't just flag the match. It'll ask: but do they actually have the problem?

That's the difference between automation that repeats and automation that learns. The first one would have scored the next similar company the same way. The second one won't.

The false positive was the most valuable output the system produced that month. Not despite being wrong. Because it was wrong.

When was the last time a tool you use actually got smarter from its own mistakes?
