## Full LinkedIn Draft

3 VCs told us to pick a "cleaner" market. We picked construction and insurance instead.

Not because we're contrarian for the sake of it.

Because we looked at where AI companies die and where they survive.

The pattern was obvious.

Sexy markets attract 200 startups in 18 months. Everyone's building the same wrapper around the same API. Differentiation comes down to marketing budget and who raised more. Product barely matters.

Ugly markets? Different game entirely.

Construction. Insurance. Logistics. Compliance.

Industries where the data is messy, the workflows are 47 steps long, the regulations change by state, and half the stakeholders don't use email.

Most founders look at that and say "pass."

Which is exactly why you shouldn't.

I spent 6 months learning how construction submittals work before we wrote a single line of code. Not because I'm patient. Because you literally can't fake domain knowledge in regulated industries. The buyer knows in 4 minutes if you understand their world or if you just read a blog post about it.

That's the moat.

Not your model. Not your UI. Not your Series A.

The fact that it took you 6 months to understand the problem well enough to solve it. That's what keeps competitors out. Because they'd need the same 6 months. And most of them won't bother when there's a shinier market two clicks away.

There's a reason enterprise AI in "boring" industries has 3x the retention rates of horizontal SaaS tools. The switching cost isn't the contract. It's the context. When your system understands that a specific insurance adjuster in Texas needs different documentation than one in Florida, that's not something a competitor can replicate by raising a seed round.

Compound knowledge. Every workflow you automate teaches you something about the next one. Every edge case you handle becomes a data point. After 12 months you don't just have a product. You have an understanding of the domain that would take a new entrant a year to match.

We see this at lumif.ai constantly. The gap between "we built an automation" and "we built an automation that actually works in this specific regulatory context" is about 8 months of painful learning.

The ugly problem advantage works like this:

Complexity filters out tourists. Regulation creates natural barriers. Domain expertise compounds. And the customers, once you earn their trust, don't leave. Because they've seen too many vendors show up with pretty demos and zero understanding of their actual constraints.

I talk to founders all the time who are agonizing over market selection. They're drawn to the clean, well-defined problems. The ones with clear APIs and modern data stacks and customers who respond to cold emails.

I get it. Those markets are easier to enter.

They're also easier for everyone else to enter.

Pick the industry that makes you slightly uncomfortable. The one where the first 3 months feel like you're learning a foreign language. Where the workflows make you question your career choices.

That discomfort is the moat being built.

If your market selection process doesn't include at least one moment of "wait, this is way more complicated than I thought," you're probably in too clean a space.

The best defensibility isn't technology. It's the willingness to sit with ugly problems long enough to actually understand them.

What's the ugliest problem in your industry that nobody wants to touch?
