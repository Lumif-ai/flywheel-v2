## Full LinkedIn Draft

8 people. 3 of them doing 5 jobs each. Admin was eating 60% of their time.

That was 14 months ago.

Today it's 10%.

Same people. Same headcount. We didn't hire anyone to "take things off their plate." We eliminated the plate.

I want to be specific about what changed because the vague version of this story is useless.

14 months ago our ops lead spent her first 2 hours every day on what I'd call productive theater. Updating trackers. Re-reading context from last week because it wasn't captured anywhere. Copying data between tools. Formatting reports nobody read past the first paragraph.

It looked like work. It felt like work. She was genuinely busy.

But none of it was her actual job.

Her actual job was building partnerships and managing client onboarding. She's exceptional at it. But she was only doing it maybe 3 hours a day because the other 5 hours went to stuff that any system could handle.

That's the team tax.

Not inefficiency. Not laziness. Not "we need better processes."

It's the cost of not having systems do what systems should do. So your best people end up as expensive middleware between tools that don't talk to each other.

We started measuring it. Across our team of 8, we found that 4 people were spending more than half their time on work that wasn't their core function. Not because they chose to. Because the infrastructure didn't exist to handle it.

The fix wasn't a new hire. It wasn't a project management tool. It wasn't a Monday morning standup about "prioritization."

It was building the connective tissue between our systems so that context didn't get lost, data didn't need manual transfer, and nobody had to re-learn what they already knew last Tuesday.

Some of it was embarrassingly simple. Auto-generating status updates from actual work output instead of asking people to write them. Pulling client data into prep docs before meetings instead of having someone spend 20 minutes assembling it manually. Routing decisions to the right person with full context instead of a Slack message that starts with "hey, quick question."

Some of it was harder. Building systems that remember what happened in a client relationship 3 months ago so you don't re-ask questions. Creating workflows where completing one task automatically sets up the next one. Making sure that when someone finishes a piece of work, the knowledge from that work is captured without them having to write a summary about it.

The 60% number from 14 months ago. That's what got me thinking about admin tax in the first place.

When your 8-person team operates like a 3-person team because 5 people's worth of effort goes to overhead, you don't have a hiring problem. You have an architecture problem.

And the brutal part is it's invisible. Because everyone's busy. Calendars are full. Slack is buzzing. Tasks are getting done. It just looks like a team at capacity.

It's not at capacity. It's at capacity for admin. The actual high-value work is fighting for scraps of time.

10% admin now. That means our 8 people operate more like, I don't know, 7 people doing their real jobs instead of 3. That's not a marginal improvement. It's a fundamentally different company.

We didn't get there by being smarter about how we do admin. We got there by making most of it unnecessary.

That's what we build at lumif.ai. Not because we think admin is evil. But because we watched our own team lose 60% of its capacity to work that had no business being done by humans.

The team tax is the most expensive line item that never shows up on your P&L.

How much of your team's time is going to work that isn't their actual job?
