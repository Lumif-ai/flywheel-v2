# Scoring Rubric Reference

Detailed scoring rubric for outreach review mode. Each message is scored on 5 dimensions,
then combined into an overall score using a weighted formula.

---

## Dimension 1: Open Likelihood (1-10)

**What it measures:** Would the subject line + first line survive the inbox? Would the
persona read past the preview pane?

| Range | Label | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1-3 | Bad | Generic subject, no reason to open | "Quick question", "Touching base", "Introduction from [Company]", "Exciting opportunity" |
| 4-6 | Mediocre | Somewhat relevant but not compelling | "Compliance tracking for [Company]", mentions industry but not specific enough to stand out |
| 7-8 | Good | Specific, relevant, earns curiosity | References a specific project, names a peer company, asks a question the persona actually thinks about |
| 9-10 | Excellent | Persona would open this even on their busiest day | Mentions something only someone who researched them would know. First line delivers on the subject promise. Pattern interrupt that fits the persona's world. |

**Key principle:** Score from the persona's perspective, not the sender's. A subject line
that sounds clever to the sender may trigger the persona's delete reflex.

---

## Dimension 2: Reply Likelihood (1-10)

**What it measures:** After reading the full message, would this persona actually type
a response? This is the highest-weighted dimension because replies are the goal.

| Range | Label | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1-3 | Bad | No reason to reply, one-directional pitch | Wall of text about the sender's product. No question asked. CTA is "let me know if interested." |
| 4-6 | Mediocre | Has a question but it is generic or low-stakes | "Would you be open to a call?" with no compelling reason. Personalization is surface-level (name + company only). |
| 7-8 | Good | Asks something the persona cares about, provides value | Shows understanding of their specific challenge. Offers a relevant insight. CTA is easy and low-commitment. |
| 9-10 | Excellent | Persona would feel compelled to respond | Identifies a problem they are actively dealing with this quarter. Offers proof from a peer they respect. CTA is so specific and low-friction they would reply even if skeptical. |

**Key principle:** People reply when the cost of replying is lower than the cost of
ignoring (they might miss something valuable).

---

## Dimension 3: Spam Signals (0-5)

**What it measures:** How many mass-blast red flags does the message contain? This is a
penalty score, not a quality score. Lower is better.

| Count | Meaning | Examples of Signals |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| 0 | Clean | No mass-blast signals detected |
| 1 | Minor | One generic element (e.g., "Dear [First Name]," with strong personalization elsewhere) |
| 2 | Noticeable | Two signals (e.g., company boilerplate paragraph + generic CTA) |
| 3 | Problematic | Reads like a template with mail-merge fields swapped |
| 4 | Severe | Multiple red flags: HTML formatting, generic opener, boilerplate body, "unsubscribe" link language |
| 5 | Mass blast | Indistinguishable from automated marketing email |

**Common spam signals to check:**
- "Dear [First Name]," as the only personalization
- Company pitch paragraph copy-pasted across all messages
- Sent from marketing-style email (noreply@, team@)
- HTML-heavy formatting (images, buttons, banners)
- "If this isn't relevant, feel free to ignore" (signals even the sender doubts relevance)
- Unsubscribe language or footer
- Multiple links
- Exclamation points in subject line
- ALL CAPS words
- Emoji in subject line for B2B outreach

---

## Dimension 4: Personalization Depth (1-10)

**What it measures:** Is there evidence of real research, or is this a name-swap template?

| Range | Label | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1-3 | Bad | Name and company swapped into a template, nothing else | "Hi Ann, I noticed BNBuilders is a leader in construction..." (could say this about any company) |
| 4-6 | Mediocre | Surface-level research, publicly obvious facts | Mentions company size or industry. References something from the homepage. Generic role-based assumptions. |
| 7-8 | Good | Specific, non-obvious research that shows effort | References a specific project, recent hire, award, news article, or industry event. Connects research to a relevant pain point. |
| 9-10 | Excellent | Research so specific the persona knows this was written for them | References their career trajectory, a specific initiative they led, a talk they gave, or a problem unique to their company's situation. Connects dots between multiple research points. |

**Key principle:** Personalization is not mentioning someone's name. It is demonstrating
understanding of their world.

---

## Dimension 5: Value Delivered (1-10)

**What it measures:** Does the recipient GET something from reading this email? Information,
insight, benchmark, data point, or perspective they didn't have before? This is the dimension
that separates "well-crafted sales email" from "email worth replying to."

| Range | Label | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1-3 | Bad | Zero value. The email only asks or pitches. | "I'd love to learn about your process." "We built X to solve Y." Pure extraction or pure pitch. |
| 4-6 | Mediocre | Vague or generic value. Industry truisms. | "Carriers are struggling with audit backlogs." True but obvious. Doesn't tell them anything new. |
| 7-8 | Good | Real insight, specific to their situation | A sourced data point they may not have seen. A pattern from peer carriers (truthful, not fabricated). A reframe of their problem they hadn't considered. |
| 9-10 | Excellent | Genuinely useful intel they'd save even if they ignore the sender | A specific, sourced benchmark relevant to their book. A regulatory change affecting their state. An offer to send something tangible (report, analysis, comparison) with no strings. |

**Key principle:** The recipient should be smarter after reading the email than before.
If they can read the entire email and learn nothing new, the value score is below 5.

**CRITICAL RULE: Every claim must be grounded.** Never fabricate statistics, benchmark
data, or "findings from carriers we've studied" unless the data is real and sourceable.
Fabricated credibility is worse than no credibility. If you don't have real data to offer,
offer a genuine question or honest framing instead. "We're early stage and building in
this space" is more credible than fake authority.

**What counts as value:**
- Publicly sourceable data point (NCCI, WCIRB, state bureau, The Institutes) applied to their specific situation
- A regulatory or market change affecting their state/book that they may not have connected to their workflow
- An offer to share something tangible (a comparison, a breakdown, a short analysis) for free
- A reframe of their problem that's genuinely novel (not just restating it back to them)
- An honest "here's what we're seeing in conversations with other carriers" (only if true)

**What does NOT count as value:**
- Asking them a question (that's extraction, not delivery)
- Restating their pain back to them ("I know audit backlogs are tough")
- Unsourced statistics ("carriers recover 18% more premium")
- "Happy to share what we're seeing" without actually sharing anything

---

## Dimension 6: CTA Effectiveness (1-10)

**What it measures:** Is the ask appropriate for this persona, at this stage of the
relationship (cold)?

| Range | Label | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1-3 | Bad | Asks too much, wrong ask, or no ask at all | "Let's schedule a 30-minute demo" (cold email, asking for 30 min from a stranger). No CTA at all (what should they do?). |
| 4-6 | Mediocre | Reasonable ask but generic or poorly framed | "Would you be open to a quick call?" (generic). "Let me know your thoughts" (vague). |
| 7-8 | Good | Low-friction, specific, matched to persona | "Worth a 10-minute look?" for a busy executive. "Can I send a 2-minute video showing how [peer] handles this?" for a visual learner. |
| 9-10 | Excellent | CTA is so well-matched the persona's default is "yes" | Offers something valuable with zero commitment. Ask is calibrated to the persona's decision authority and tech adoption posture. Makes replying easier than ignoring. |

**Key principle:** Cold email CTAs should match the temperature of the relationship.
First touch = lowest possible friction. Asking a stranger for 30 minutes is like
proposing marriage on a first date.

---

## Overall Score Formula

```
Overall = (Open * 0.15) + (Reply * 0.20) + ((10 - Spam * 2) * 0.10) + (Personalization * 0.10) + (Value * 0.30) + (CTA * 0.15)
```

**Why these weights:**
- **Value (0.30):** THE most important dimension. Without value, nothing else matters. A beautifully crafted, personalized, pattern-breaking email that offers the recipient nothing will not get a reply. This is the lesson from batch 1 (zero replies despite decent personalization in follow-ups).
- **Reply (0.20):** The ultimate goal, but reply is a CONSEQUENCE of value delivered, not independent of it.
- **Open (0.15):** If they do not open, nothing else matters. But opening without value is a wasted open.
- **CTA (0.15):** The mechanism of reply. Must match the value offered.
- **Spam signals (0.10):** Converted to a 0-10 scale via `(10 - Spam * 2)`. Reduced weight because if value is high, minor spam signals are forgiven.
- **Personalization (0.10):** Important but not sufficient alone. Reduced weight because deep personalization without value is just flattery.

**The key insight:** The previous 5-dimension rubric could produce a 9.0 score on a message that offered the recipient NOTHING. That's a rubric failure. Value is now the heaviest weight because it's the only dimension that gives the recipient a reason to engage.

---

## Thresholds

| Score Range | Verdict | Action |
|-------------|---------|--------|
| 7.0+ | **Pass** | Message is ready to send. |
| 5.0 - 6.9 | **Auto-redraft** | Redraft once using persona + advisor guidance. |
| Below 5.0 | **Flag** | Do not auto-redraft. Flag for manual review. Message likely needs strategic rethink, not wordsmithing. |

---

## Auto-Redraft Process

When a message scores 5.0-6.9, the skill auto-redrafts it once. Here is how:

### Step 1: Diagnose

Identify the weakest dimensions (lowest scores). These drive the rewrite priorities.

### Step 2: Load Persona Context

- Read the persona's JTBD section. The rewrite must address what they actually care about.
- Read open triggers and delete triggers. The rewrite must use the former and avoid the latter.
- Read the language section. The rewrite must use their vocabulary, not vendor-speak.
- Read effective patterns (opener, CTA, length, tone). Follow these constraints.

### Step 3: Apply Advisor Frameworks

Based on the weakest dimensions, apply the most relevant advisor framework:

| Weak Dimension | Primary Advisor | Framework Applied |
|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Open Likelihood | Becc Holland | Pattern interrupt opener. Break the expected email format. |
| Reply Likelihood | Josh Braun | Curiosity-based approach. "Poke the bear" -- surface a problem they have not articulated yet. |
| Spam Signals | Kyle Coleman | CROP framework. Ensure every sentence has Context, Relevance, Outcome, or Proof. Remove anything that does not. |
| Personalization | Sam McKenna | "Show me you know me." Replace generic references with specific research points. |
| CTA | Alex Hormozi | Value equation. Make the offer so good (and low-risk) they feel foolish saying no. |

### Step 4: Rewrite

Rewrite the message following persona constraints and advisor guidance. Produce a single
new draft. Do NOT re-score or enter a review loop.

### Step 5: Output

Show the original message, the scores, the redrafted message, and a brief explanation
of what changed and why.

---

## Calibration Notes

The 7.0 threshold is a heuristic starting point. It will be calibrated as response data
accumulates through feedback mode. Key calibration questions:

- Do messages scoring 7.0-7.5 actually get responses? If not, raise the bar.
- Do messages scoring 6.0-6.9 get responses after redraft? If frequently, the redraft is working.
- Are there dimensions that correlate more strongly with actual responses? If so, adjust weights.

The scoring formula, weights, and thresholds are all tunable. Changes are recorded in
the skill's memory file and changelog.
